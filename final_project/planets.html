Diversity of Worlds

There are eight planets in our solar system, along with five dwarf planets. They are all part of the Orion Arm, which is one of the outer branches of the Milky Way galaxy. These planets are very different from each other—Mercury is small and covered in craters, while Neptune is freezing cold with powerful winds. Besides the planets, there are also over 290 moons, more than 1.3 million asteroids, and about 3,900 comets in the solar system.

Inner Terrestrial Planets

Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars are the four planets closest to the Sun. They all have solid, rocky surfaces made of rock and metal. Mercury is the smallest planet and has almost no atmosphere, so its temperature changes a lot between day and night. Venus is close to Earth in size but much hotter because thick clouds trap the heat. Earth is the only planet with liquid water on the surface. Mars has a reddish color because of iron oxide (rust) in its soil, and scientists have found evidence of ancient rivers and ice caps there.

Outer Gas & Ice Giants

Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune are much larger and do not have solid surfaces. Instead, they are made of thick layers of gas and liquid around a dense core. Jupiter is the biggest planet and has a famous storm called the Great Red Spot, which is larger than Earth. Saturn is known for its rings, which are made of ice and rock. Uranus and Neptune are called ice giants. Uranus spins on its side because of its extreme tilt, and Neptune has the fastest winds of any planet in the solar system.
